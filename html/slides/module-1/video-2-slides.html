The Problem Without Licenses

  • ‚ùå Unclear what users can legally do
  • ‚ùå Code might be "stolen" and closed
  • ‚ùå No protection for contributors
  • ‚ùå Legal uncertainty prevents adoption
  • ‚ùå Collaboration becomes impossible

‚ö†Ô∏è Without a license = All Rights Reserved

"All Rights Reserved" = Default copyright
Nobody can use, modify, or distribute (even if public!)

So if open source is about sharing, why do we need licenses? Because without a license, code is automatically under exclusive copyright‚Äî"All Rights Reserved" means nobody can legally use it, even if you posted it publicly on GitHub. Licenses don't restrict freedom; they enable it by providing clarity and legal protection.

Licenses Are ENABLERS, Not Barriers

  • ‚úì Clarity - Explicit permissions & obligations
  • ‚úì Protection - For creators AND users
  • ‚úì Trust - Known rules for collaboration
  • ‚úì Compatibility - What can be combined
  • ‚úì Legal Standing - Foundation if needed

üí° Think: "Rules of the road" for collaboration

Licenses provide clarity, protection, trust, compatibility, and legal standing. They're like the rules of the road‚Äîthey don't prevent you from driving, they make safe driving possible. Same with open source collaboration.

The License Spectrum

Public Domain ‚Üê Permissive ‚Üê Weak Copyleft ‚Üê Strong Copyleft
    (CC0)         (MIT, BSD)     (LGPL, MPL)      (GPL, AGPL)

Most Permissive ‚Üê‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚Üí Most Protective

üí° Copyleft = "Share-alike" requirement
If you distribute modified versions, you must share them under the same license

All allow commercial use!

Differ in sharing requirements for modifications

Open source licenses exist on a spectrum from most permissive to most protective. On the left, permissive licenses like MIT and BSD have minimal restrictions. On the right, copyleft licenses like GPL ensure derivatives remain open through a "share-alike" requirement. Important: they ALL allow commercial use! The difference is whether you must share modifications when you distribute them. Copyleft means if you distribute a modified version, you must share it under the same open license.

Permissive Licenses: MIT, BSD, Apache

  • ‚úÖ Minimal restrictions
  • ‚úÖ Can be used in proprietary software
  • ‚úÖ Main requirement: Attribution (give credit)
  • ‚úÖ Maximum adoption strategy

Examples:

  • Leaflet (web mapping) - BSD
  • pandas (data analysis) - BSD
  • React (Facebook) - MIT

üíº Companies love these: Low friction

Permissive licenses like MIT are incredibly simple. You can use the code commercially, modify it, even incorporate it into proprietary software. The main requirement? Give credit. That's it. This is why companies love permissive licenses‚Äîlow friction for adoption. Leaflet, pandas, React‚Äîall use permissive licenses to maximize their reach.

Copyleft Licenses: GPL, AGPL

  • ‚úÖ "Share-alike" philosophy
  • ‚úÖ Derivatives must remain open
  • ‚úÖ Protects user freedoms forever
  • ‚úÖ Community protection strategy

Examples:

  • QGIS - GPL v2
  • PostGIS - GPL v2
  • Linux kernel - GPL v2

üí° Commercial use allowed!
Restriction: Must share modifications if distributed

Copyleft licenses take a different approach. The philosophy is 'share-alike'‚Äîif you distribute modified versions, you must share them under the same license. This ensures the software stays open forever. QGIS uses GPL so no company can take it, modify it, and sell a closed version. But notice: commercial use is absolutely allowed! Red Hat makes billions selling Linux support. The restriction is about sharing modifications, not making money.

Case Study: QGIS - GPL v2

Why GPL?

  • Ensures QGIS stays open forever
  • All plugins must be GPL-compatible
  • Community investment protected

Can you...

  • ‚úÖ Use commercially? YES
  • ‚úÖ Charge for QGIS? YES
  • ‚úÖ Modify it? YES
  • ‚ö†Ô∏è Distribute modifications? Must share source

Strategy: Protect the commons

Let's look at QGIS's license choice. They chose GPL to ensure the entire ecosystem‚Äîcore software and plugins‚Äîremains open. Can you use QGIS commercially? Absolutely. Many companies do. Can you charge for QGIS support or services? Yes! The only restriction: if you distribute a modified version, you must share the source code. This protects the community's investment.

Case Study: Leaflet - BSD 2-Clause

Why BSD?

  • Maximize adoption everywhere
  • Minimal barriers for companies
  • Enable proprietary products built on top

Used by:

  • Mapbox (commercial)
  • CARTO (commercial)
  • Facebook, GitHub, NPR

Strategy: Ubiquity through simplicity

Leaflet chose BSD‚Äîvery permissive. Why? To maximize adoption. Companies like Mapbox and CARTO build proprietary products on top of Leaflet, and that's fine. The goal is to be everywhere. Different strategy, equally valid.

Case Study: GDAL - MIT/X License

Why MIT?

  • Be the universal foundation
  • Used by everyone, everywhere
  • Broad integration across sectors

Users include:

  • NASA, Google, Planet Labs
  • Esri (proprietary GIS)
  • Open source projects
  • Academic institutions

Strategy: Universal infrastructure

GDAL chose MIT to be the universal geospatial foundation. NASA uses it. Google uses it. Even Esri uses it in their proprietary software. Commercial and non-profit sectors both contribute because the permissive license enables broad integration. Different goals require different license strategies.
